There is a familiar story about the relationship between money and politics. It is a story about corruption — about individuals who broke the rules, took the bribes, made the deals they shouldn't have made. It is a story about deviation: a healthy system infected by bad actors, a problem that better enforcement, stricter regulation, greater transparency could in principle correct.

This is not that story.

What follows is an examination of the relationship between economic power and political power in liberal democracies — not at its worst, but at its most normal. We are not, in this paper, concerned with what happens in the shadows: with dark money networks, with the frankly criminal dimensions of the overlap between concentrated wealth and political influence. Those things exist, they matter, and we will address them in due course. But we set them aside here deliberately, because we want to establish something more unsettling first: that the problem we are describing operates entirely in the open, within the law, through mechanisms that are widely understood, widely practised, and in many cases actively celebrated as features of a healthy democratic system.

Strip away every corrupt act, prosecute every bad actor, close every loophole — and the structural problem we are about to describe remains intact. That is the claim. We will now make the case for it.

The analysis developed here is part of a broader project that treats the economic structure of society not as a sovereign cause from which all political and social forms mechanically follow, but as a selection environment — one that makes certain institutional arrangements more stable, certain careers more viable, certain policy outcomes more probable, without determining any of them with necessity. The mode of production constrains and drives social development without predetermining it; it defines the space of what is possible, and within that space, human choices, institutional designs, and political intelligence remain genuine causal forces. On the traditional language of base and superstructure, the argument is not that the economic base dictates the political superstructure, but that it structures the field within which political forms emerge, compete, and either stabilise or fail. What follows is an account of how that structuring operates — specifically, through the selection pressures that the economic system applies to political life.

The System Working as Designed

Political careers in liberal democracies require backing. This is not a cynical observation — it is a structural one, and it holds across the considerable variation in how different democracies organise their politics. Resources are needed: money for campaigns, access to media, the endorsement of credible institutions, networks that open doors and smooth paths. The specific form those resources take varies — American politics is dominated by direct fundraising in ways that Westminster or Scandinavian systems are not — but the underlying dependency on organised support does not disappear when the mechanism changes. It relocates. Where individual candidates do not raise money directly, parties do; and the economic interests that might otherwise fund candidates instead fund parties, cultivate relationships with party leaderships, and shape — through candidate selection pressures, policy pipelines, and the think tanks and advisory ecosystems that feed political culture — the internal criteria through which careers are made. The filter moves upstream. The dependency does not dissolve — it becomes less visible.

It is worth acknowledging that some political systems have made serious attempts to attenuate this dependency through public financing of parties, strict spending limits, and robust campaign finance regulation. These efforts are not meaningless — where they have been most comprehensively implemented they do genuinely reduce the direct purchase of political access. But they often come under sustained pressure from the very interests whose influence they constrain, and they address the most visible mechanism of economic influence without touching the deeper structural relationship we are about to describe. Money is only one of the currencies through which economic power shapes political outcomes. Social networks, media access, the prestige of institutional endorsement, and the sheer density of relationships that concentrate around established economic interests — these do not disappear when direct financial routes are regulated.

The backing that matters most is, in most cases, systematically concentrated among those who are already economically powerful — established business interests, financial institutions, major property owners, the controllers of media. Proximity to these networks is not evenly distributed across society; it correlates strongly with class, education, and prior economic position, which means that the filtering process begins well before any deliberate cultivation of political relationships takes place. Those who lack it must build it from scratch — and building it has terms: of time, access, and adaptation to the expectations of those who already control it.

This is not the whole picture. Organised labour, mass membership parties, and grassroots movements represent genuine countervailing forces, and their historical importance in shaping the political economy of the twentieth century cannot be overstated. But the structural asymmetry between these forces and concentrated economic interests has widened dramatically over the past four decades — driven by capital mobility that outpaced the reach of nationally organised labour, the managed decline of union membership across most liberal democracies, and the consolidation of media ownership into progressively fewer and larger economic entities. That widening is not incidental to the argument we are making. It is, as we will show, one of its outputs — the selection environment operating over time, progressively favouring some configurations of power and penalising others.

The Asymmetry

The dependency described above does not run equally in both directions, and the inequality is not merely one of degree. The relationship between an aspiring political actor and an established interest group is not a negotiation between parties of comparable standing. It is a relationship between an entity that can survive — and readily replace — the loss of any individual political ally, and an individual whose viability may depend critically on maintaining that entity's support.

The structural consequence of this asymmetry goes beyond the psychology of obligation and gratitude, though those are real enough. Consider what the pathway to office actually selects for. It favours, first, those with prior social proximity to the networks that matter — who know the right people, move in the right circles, are already legible to the gatekeepers of political viability. It favours, second, those with the temperamental comfort to operate within those networks on their implicit terms — who do not find the dependency troubling, or who have learned not to show it. It favours, third, those whose prior economic position makes the relationships feel reciprocal rather than transactional — who bring something to the table that makes the cultivation feel mutual. Those who lack these qualities are not, in most cases, persuaded or corrupted after the fact. They are screened out earlier, at the stages of selection, funding, and endorsement, before they are close enough to power for their independence to matter.

This is, in the precise sense the Foundation has used elsewhere, a selection mechanism. The political system does not randomly sample from the population of potential leaders and subsequently compromise them. It applies differential pressure across the full pathway to power — rewarding certain configurations of social position, temperament, and prior alignment, penalising others — and the political class that results reflects that accumulated pressure. The process is continuous with the economic selection mechanism described in this project's earlier analysis: not conspiracy, not deliberate design, but the aggregate effect of a structured environment operating on the choices available to agents within it. The same logic that shapes who rises in a corporation shapes who reaches the threshold of political viability. The two selection environments are not identical, but they are connected — and the connection runs through the dependency relationships described above.

This is the distinction that deserves emphasis. The concern is not primarily that politicians arrive in office with genuine independence and are subsequently compromised — though that happens. The more structurally significant phenomenon is that the pathway to office itself operates as a filter, such that those who arrive are, in the main, already aligned. The sympathies were not cultivated in office. They were the condition of arrival.

That politicians who break with their backers once in office exist is not a refutation of this argument — it is evidence for it. Such cases are notable precisely because they are rare, and their careers are often subsequently circumscribed in ways that illustrate the persistence of the filtering mechanism rather than its absence.

The asymmetry is sharpest at the moment it matters most — early in a career, before office is held and before formal power provides any countervailing leverage. At this stage, the aspiring politician has everything to gain from the relationship and little to offer beyond future alignment. The interest group, by contrast, is operating across many such relationships simultaneously, can afford to back multiple candidates, and can redirect its support if a given individual proves unreliable. The individual politician is not substitutable in the same way — their career depends on this network of relationships in a way that no single relationship within the network depends on them.

Once in office, the formal powers of political actors do provide genuine leverage — votes, appointments, agenda-setting, regulatory decisions. A politician is not simply an asset to be deployed. But two things constrain how that leverage is used. First, the filtering process means that those who have reached office through the conventional pathway are already, in the main, broadly sympathetic to the interests that supported their ascent — not through corruption but through the entirely ordinary processes of socialisation, network formation, and the gradual alignment of perspective that comes from years of operating within a particular set of relationships. Second, the prospect of future support — for re-election, for post-political careers, for the maintenance of the networks that make political life functional — extends the dependency forward in time. The asymmetry that shaped the entry to power continues to shape its exercise.

The Feedback

The dependency and filtering described above would matter less if political outcomes were determined primarily by democratic deliberation — by argument, evidence, and the preferences of the broad electorate. The claim being made here is that they are not, and that this is a persistent tendency under conditions of concentrated economic power rather than an occasional aberration. The systematic bias introduced by the concentration of politically valuable resources produces, over time, a predictable skew in policy outcomes: toward the preferences of those with the most access, and away from those without it.

This claim has been tested empirically, most rigorously in the American context. The political scientist Martin Gilens, in Affluence and Influence (Princeton University Press, 2012), analysed the relationship between the policy preferences of different income groups and actual policy outcomes across hundreds of policy questions over several decades. His finding was stark: when the preferences of median-income voters diverged from those of economic elites, policy followed elite preferences at a rate statistically indistinguishable from what you would expect if median voter preferences had no independent effect at all. Jacob Hacker and Paul Pierson, in Winner-Take-All Politics (Simon & Schuster, 2010), document the same pattern from a different direction — tracing decades of policy drift that has concentrated economic gains at the top of the income distribution, and showing how that drift tracks the preferences of organised economic interests rather than the general population. The claim is about independent influence conditional on disagreement — not merely that elites and policymakers often agree, which might reflect shared information or aligned interests, but that when they diverge, the divergence is resolved systematically in one direction.

These studies concern the United States, and the specifics of American campaign finance make the mechanisms there unusually visible. But the underlying structural logic is not American — it operates wherever political advancement requires cultivation of networks and resources systematically controlled by concentrated economic interests. Parliamentary systems and candidate-centred ones distribute the dependency differently; they do not eliminate it. The mechanism changes form across different institutional settings. The directional bias does not.

The systematic bias introduced by concentrated economic power does not require anyone to be acting in bad faith. It requires only that the normal operation of political life continues — that careers are built, relationships are maintained, resources flow toward those who have demonstrated reliable sympathy. The outcomes follow from the structure, not from the intentions of the individuals within it.

The structural argument

The Capture of Implementation

The structural bias described above operates most visibly at the level of policy formation — shaping what decisions are made. But it operates at a second level that receives less attention and is in some respects more alarming: the level of policy implementation. Democratic governments do not merely decide policy. They depend on the institutions, industries, and actors through which policy must be transmitted in order for it to take effect. When those transmission mechanisms are controlled by interests whose incentives diverge from the policy's goals, the policy can be formally enacted and practically neutralised simultaneously — not through conspiracy, but through the entirely rational behaviour of institutions operating within their own incentive structures.

The period following the 2008 financial crisis provides a case study in both dimensions. The political relationships that the financial sector had built over decades proved, first, sufficient to prevent the fundamental regulatory reform that the scale of the crisis would plausibly have been expected to produce. The sector had caused a crisis that required public bailouts costing tens of billions of pounds in the UK alone — and emerged with its basic operating model and political relationships broadly intact. The regulatory changes that followed were real but partial; the structural features of the financial system that had produced the crisis were modified at the margins rather than addressed at their roots. The accumulated political capital of the financial sector is a significant part of any adequate explanation of that outcome.

But there is a second, less visible dimension of the same story. Following the crisis, the Bank of England pursued a dramatic loosening of monetary policy — cutting the base rate from 5% in April 2008 to 0.5% by March 2009, and subsequently launching a programme of quantitative easing that would eventually involve the purchase of £375 billion of assets. The stated purpose of these interventions was to stimulate the real economy: to reduce borrowing costs for households and businesses, to increase the flow of credit, and to support productive economic activity.

What the data shows is that the transmission of this policy through the banking sector was substantially incomplete — and incomplete in ways that aligned with the sector's own commercial interests rather than the policy's stated goals. Evidence submitted to parliamentary inquiries on the effectiveness of post-crisis monetary policy documents the pattern: while base rates fell to historic lows, the pass-through to lending rates across different credit products was highly uneven. Lending to non-financial businesses remained negative — more loans repaid than issued — for years after the emergency rate cuts began. Meanwhile, the composition of consumer lending shifted: the cheap liquidity provided to banks flowed disproportionately not into the productive lending the policy was designed to stimulate, but toward higher-margin consumer credit products where institutional returns were more attractive. The availability of cheap central bank funding did not, in other words, produce a corresponding cheapening of the credit products through which ordinary households were most exposed to the financial system.

It would be a mistake to read this as coordinated defiance of government policy. The banks were not, in any meaningful sense, choosing to subvert the Bank of England's intentions. They were responding to the incentive structures they faced — structures in which high-margin consumer credit was more profitable than low-margin mortgage or business lending, and in which the regulatory framework imposed insufficient pressure to alter that calculation. But the incentive structures they faced were precisely those that a more robust regulatory settlement — one that had not been progressively shaped, over decades, by the political relationships described in this paper — might have looked quite different. The policy was transmitted through institutions whose commercial logic redirected it. The result was that an instrument of democratic economic governance was systematically deflected by the operational behaviour of the institutions through which it had to work. Policy was made; its implementation was captured by structure.

The Sedimentation

What makes the dynamic described above more than a static problem is that it is a self-reinforcing one. Each cycle does not merely reproduce the existing distribution of political and economic power — it extends and consolidates it, progressively narrowing the space within which countervailing forces can operate.

The mechanism is straightforward once named. Policy outcomes that reflect the preferences of concentrated economic interests tend, over time, to increase the relative economic weight of those interests. Greater economic weight translates, in the next cycle, into greater political access and more resources to deploy in cultivating it. Greater political access produces outcomes that further extend the advantage. The feedback does not dampen — it amplifies. And because it amplifies gradually, through the normal operation of ordinary political and economic life, it does not announce itself as a crisis. It accumulates.

The post-crisis period illustrates the sedimentation effect concretely. The combination of near-zero interest rates, quantitative easing, and the uneven credit distribution described above produced a sharply skewed distributional outcome. Bank of England analysis cited in parliamentary evidence found that the rise in household wealth between 2007 and 2014 accrued overwhelmingly to older, already-asset-rich households, while younger households without existing asset holdings saw their relative position decline. This was not a random distributional outcome — it was the predictable result of cheap liquidity flowing toward asset price inflation rather than productive investment, in a context where the regulatory framework permitted that redirection. Those who held assets at the start of the period held more at the end. Those who did not, fell further behind.

The same dynamic, operating over a longer timeframe and across multiple domains — taxation, housing policy, financial regulation, labour law — produces the sedimentation effect proper: not the reproduction of inequality but its progressive deepening, and with it the progressive deepening of the structural conditions that make corrective democratic governance harder to perform. The pool from which political actors are drawn, the networks through which careers are built, the media through which political narratives are shaped — all are affected by the underlying distribution of economic power, which the feedback loop is continuously and directionally shifting. Each cycle leaves the selection environment slightly more tilted than the last. The capture becomes progressively more complete; the alternatives become progressively more marginal; and the structural bias becomes progressively harder to perceive as a problem rather than a natural order, because it has been progressively normalised by the institutions that reflect it.

The sedimentation effect does not produce a crisis. It produces a condition — one in which the gap between what democratic institutions are supposed to do and what they are structurally capable of doing widens, quietly and continuously, until the distance becomes impossible to ignore. By the time it is impossible to ignore, it has become very hard to close.

The long-run danger

What This Does Not Primarily Depend On

Before proceeding, it is worth being precise about what the argument so far has not primarily depended on. It has not depended on any individual actor behaving with corrupt intent. It has not assumed that interest groups are deliberately seeking to subvert democracy rather than simply pursuing their interests through the mechanisms available to them. It has not assumed bad faith anywhere in the system. The mechanism can operate — and in the main does operate — through entirely ordinary human behaviour: the cultivation of useful relationships, the maintenance of loyalties, the natural drift of perception toward the perspectives of those one spends time with and depends upon.

It operates through the legal exercise of rights that are, in most liberal democracies, considered fundamental: the right to organise politically, to spend money in pursuit of political goals, to advocate for your interests through the institutions of democratic life. The think tank that shapes the policy environment, the donor who funds the party, the former regulator who joins the industry they once oversaw — none of these need be acting in bad faith. The structure produces its outcomes through the aggregation of individually defensible choices.

This is the point that must be held clearly as the analysis proceeds. We are not describing a system that has been corrupted. We are describing a system that is producing, through its normal operation, outcomes systematically biased toward existing concentrations of power — and that is, in doing so, undermining the regulatory and corrective capacity that we need it to exercise. The selection pressures described here constrain the range of viable options available to political actors operating within the existing structure; they do not fully determine which option is taken in any given moment. Actors within the system make real choices, and those choices have real consequences — some resist the structural pressure, and some, through accumulated effort and political intelligence, change the rules from which that pressure flows. The mode of production is, as this project has argued elsewhere, a set of rules — and rules can be changed.

What happens when criminal and quasi-criminal behaviour is introduced into this structure — the darker layers that this paper has set aside — is a subject for the analysis that follows. The reader should carry into that analysis the understanding established here: that the foundation on which those behaviours operate is not a healthy system being exploited. It is a structurally compromised one. The shadows are darker for what the light already shows.
