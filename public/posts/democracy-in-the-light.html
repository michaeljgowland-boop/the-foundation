There is a familiar story about the relationship between money and politics. It is a story about corruption — about individuals who broke the rules, took the bribes, made the deals they shouldn't have made. It is a story about deviation: a healthy system infected by bad actors, a problem that better enforcement, stricter regulation, greater transparency could in principle correct.

This is not that story.

What follows is an examination of the relationship between economic power and political power in liberal democracies — not at its worst, but at its most normal. We are not, in this paper, concerned with what happens in the shadows: with dark money networks, with the frankly criminal dimensions of the overlap between concentrated wealth and political influence. Those things exist, they matter, and we will address them in due course. But we set them aside here deliberately, because we want to establish something more unsettling first: that the problem we are describing operates entirely in the open, within the law, through mechanisms that are widely understood, widely practised, and in many cases actively celebrated as features of a healthy democratic system.

Strip away every corrupt act, prosecute every bad actor, close every loophole — and the structural problem we are about to describe remains intact. That is the claim. We will now make the case for it.

The analysis that follows sits within a broader project that treats the economic structure of society not as a sovereign cause from which political and social forms mechanically follow, but as a selection environment — one that makes certain institutional arrangements more stable, certain careers more viable, certain policy outcomes more probable, without determining any of them with necessity. The mode of production constrains and drives social development without predetermining it. Within the space it defines, human choices, institutional designs, and political intelligence remain genuine causal forces. This is not a qualification offered to soften the argument — it is the argument's foundation. If structural forces determined outcomes completely, this analysis would be pointless. They do not, which is precisely why it matters.

The System Working as Designed

Political careers in liberal democracies require backing. This is not a cynical observation — it is a structural one, and it holds across the considerable variation in how different democracies organise their politics. Resources are needed: money for campaigns, access to media, the endorsement of credible institutions, networks that open doors and smooth paths. The specific form those resources take varies — American politics is dominated by direct fundraising in ways that Westminster or Scandinavian systems are not — but the underlying dependency on organised support does not disappear when the mechanism changes. It relocates. Where individual candidates do not raise money directly, parties do; and the economic interests that might otherwise fund candidates instead fund parties, cultivate relationships with party leaderships, and shape — through candidate selection pressures, policy pipelines, and the think tanks and advisory ecosystems that feed political culture — the internal criteria through which careers are made. The filter moves upstream. The dependency does not dissolve — it becomes less visible.

It is worth acknowledging that some political systems have made serious attempts to attenuate this dependency through public financing of parties, strict spending limits, and robust campaign finance regulation. These efforts are not meaningless — where they have been most comprehensively implemented they do genuinely reduce the direct purchase of political access. But they often come under sustained pressure from the very interests whose influence they constrain, and they address the most visible mechanism of economic influence without touching the deeper structural relationship we are about to describe. Money is only one of the currencies through which economic power shapes political outcomes. Social networks, media access, the prestige of institutional endorsement, and the sheer density of relationships that concentrate around established economic interests — these do not disappear when direct financial routes are regulated.

The backing that matters most is, in most cases, systematically concentrated among those who are already economically powerful — established business interests, financial institutions, major property owners, the controllers of media. Proximity to these networks is not evenly distributed across society; it correlates strongly with class, education, and prior economic position, which means that the filtering process begins well before any deliberate cultivation of political relationships takes place. Those who lack it must build it from scratch — and building it has terms: of time, access, and adaptation to the expectations of those who already control it.

This is not the whole picture. Organised labour, mass membership parties, and grassroots movements represent genuine countervailing forces, and their historical importance in shaping the political economy of the twentieth century cannot be overstated. But the structural asymmetry between these forces and concentrated economic interests has widened dramatically over the past four decades — driven by capital mobility that outpaced the reach of nationally organised labour, the managed decline of union membership across most liberal democracies, and the consolidation of media ownership into progressively fewer and larger economic entities. That widening is not incidental to the argument we are making. It is, as we will show, one of its outputs — the feedback loop operating over time.

The Asymmetry

The dependency described above does not run equally in both directions, and the inequality is not merely one of degree. The relationship between an aspiring political actor and an established interest group is not a negotiation between parties of comparable standing. It is a relationship between an entity that can survive — and readily replace — the loss of any individual political ally, and an individual whose viability may depend critically on maintaining that entity's support.

This asymmetry has a structural consequence that goes well beyond the psychology of obligation and gratitude, though those are real enough. The more fundamental mechanism is one of selection. Consider what the pathway to office actually selects for. It favours, first, those with prior social proximity to the networks that matter — who know the right people, move in the right circles, are already legible to the gatekeepers of political viability. It favours, second, those with the temperamental comfort to operate within those networks on their implicit terms — who do not find the dependency troubling, or who have learned not to show it. It favours, third, those whose prior economic position makes the relationships feel reciprocal rather than transactional — who bring something to the table that makes the cultivation feel mutual. Those who lack these qualities are not, in most cases, persuaded or corrupted after the fact. They are screened out earlier — at the stages of selection, funding, and endorsement — before they are close enough to power for their independence to matter.

The political system, in other words, does not randomly sample from the population of potential leaders and subsequently bend them to its interests. It applies differential pressure across the full pathway to power, rewarding certain configurations of social position, temperament, and prior alignment, and penalising others. The political class that results reflects that pressure — continuously and as a matter of structure, not exception. This is the same selection logic the Foundation has identified operating within the economic system: not conspiracy, not deliberate design, but the aggregate effect of a structured environment acting on the choices available to those within it. The filter built into the economic system and the filter built into the political pathway are not independent of each other. They are, as we will show, aspects of the same system.

This is the distinction that deserves emphasis. The concern is not primarily that politicians arrive in office with genuine independence and are subsequently compromised — though that happens. The more structurally significant phenomenon is that the pathway to office itself operates as a filter, such that those who arrive are, in the main, already aligned. The sympathies were not cultivated in office. They were the condition of arrival.

That politicians who break with their backers once in office exist is not a refutation of this argument — it is evidence for it. Such cases are notable precisely because they are rare, and their careers are often subsequently circumscribed in ways that illustrate the persistence of the selection mechanism rather than its absence.

The asymmetry is sharpest at the moment it matters most — early in a career, before office is held and before formal power provides any countervailing leverage. At this stage, the aspiring politician has everything to gain from the relationship and little to offer beyond future alignment. The interest group, by contrast, is operating across many such relationships simultaneously, can afford to back multiple candidates, and can redirect its support if a given individual proves unreliable. The individual politician is not substitutable in the same way — their career depends on this network of relationships in a way that no single relationship within the network depends on them.

Once in office, the formal powers of political actors do provide genuine leverage — votes, appointments, agenda-setting, regulatory decisions. A politician is not simply an asset to be deployed. But two things constrain how that leverage is used. First, the selection process means that those who have reached office through the conventional pathway are already, in the main, broadly sympathetic to the interests that supported their ascent — not through corruption but through the entirely ordinary processes of socialisation, network formation, and the gradual alignment of perspective that comes from years of operating within a particular set of relationships. Second, the prospect of future support — for re-election, for post-political careers, for the maintenance of the networks that make political life functional — extends the dependency forward in time. The asymmetry that shaped the entry to power continues to shape its exercise.

The Feedback

The dependency and selection described above would matter less if political outcomes were determined primarily by democratic deliberation — by argument, evidence, and the preferences of the broad electorate. The claim being made here is that they are not. The systematic bias introduced by the concentration of politically valuable resources produces, over time, a predictable skew in policy outcomes: toward the preferences of those with the most access, and away from those without it.

This claim should be distinguished carefully from a simpler one. We are not arguing that economic elites get what they want because they share their preferences with policymakers, or because their views are better informed, or because their interests happen to align with economic growth. Each of those alternative explanations has some force. The claim is more specific: that economic interests exercise independent influence on policy outcomes, over and above any alignment of preference — and that this independent influence is traceable to the structural position they occupy in the political pathway we have described.

This claim has been tested empirically, most rigorously in the American context. The political scientist Martin Gilens, in Affluence and Influence (Princeton University Press, 2012), analysed the relationship between the policy preferences of different income groups and actual policy outcomes across hundreds of policy questions over several decades. His finding was stark: when the preferences of median-income voters diverged from those of economic elites, policy followed elite preferences at a rate statistically indistinguishable from what you would expect if median voter preferences had no independent effect at all. Jacob Hacker and Paul Pierson, in Winner-Take-All Politics (Simon & Schuster, 2010), document the same pattern from a different direction — tracing decades of policy drift that has concentrated economic gains at the top of the income distribution, and showing how that drift tracks the preferences of organised economic interests rather than the general population.

These studies concern the United States, and the specifics of American campaign finance make the mechanisms there unusually visible. Parliamentary systems, party discipline, and varying campaign finance regimes mean that the institutional details differ significantly across liberal democracies. But the underlying structural logic is not American — it operates wherever political advancement requires cultivation of networks and resources systematically controlled by concentrated economic interests, which is to say wherever parties depend on funding structures, wherever careers are made through networks, and wherever the policy advisory ecosystem is populated disproportionately by those with existing economic stakes in its outputs. The mechanism changes form; the directional bias does not.

The Capture of Implementation

The structural bias described above operates most visibly at the level of policy formation — shaping what decisions are made. But it operates at a second level that receives less attention and is in some respects more alarming: the level of implementation. Democratic governments do not merely decide policy. They depend on the institutions, industries, and actors through which policy must be transmitted in order for it to take effect. When those transmission mechanisms are controlled by interests whose incentives diverge from the policy's goals, the policy can be formally enacted and practically neutralised simultaneously — not through any act of obstruction, but simply through the aggregate effect of rational self-interested behaviour within an inadequately constrained system.

The period following the 2008 financial crisis provides a case study in both dimensions. The political relationships that the financial sector had built over decades proved, first, strong enough to prevent the fundamental regulatory reform that the scale of the crisis would plausibly have been expected to produce. The sector had caused a crisis that required public bailouts costing tens of billions of pounds in the UK alone — had to be rescued by the very democratic institutions it had systematically worked to prevent from constraining it — and emerged with its basic operating model and political relationships broadly intact. The regulatory changes introduced in response — Basel III capital requirements, stress testing regimes, partial structural reforms — were real, but they modified the system at the margins rather than addressing the structural features that had produced the crisis. This outcome is difficult to explain on any account that does not include the accumulated political capital of the financial sector as a significant variable.

But there is a second, less visible dimension of the same story — one that illustrates how structural power operates not just on the formation of policy but on its transmission. Following the crisis, the Bank of England pursued a dramatic loosening of monetary policy, cutting the base rate from 5% in April 2008 to 0.5% by March 2009, and subsequently launching a programme of quantitative easing that would eventually involve the purchase of £375 billion of assets. The stated purpose was to stimulate the real economy: to reduce borrowing costs, increase the flow of credit, and support productive economic activity.

What the data shows is that the pass-through of this policy through the banking sector was substantially incomplete — and incomplete in ways that aligned with the sector's own balance sheet priorities rather than the policy's stated goals. Evidence submitted to parliamentary inquiries on the effectiveness of post-crisis monetary policy documents the pattern clearly: net lending to non-financial businesses remained negative — with more loans being repaid than issued — until 2016, seven years after the emergency rate cuts began. The cheap liquidity provided to banks flowed disproportionately not into the productive lending the policy was designed to stimulate, but toward higher-margin consumer credit products and asset markets, where returns were more attractive to the institutions managing the funds. As parliamentary evidence noted, the cheap availability of credit to major financial institutions was not consistently passed on, as those institutions prioritised recapitalisation and margin improvement on new lending.

It would be too simple to describe this as deliberate subversion. The demand-side environment was also weak — post-crisis deleveraging, risk aversion among businesses, and regulatory pressures on bank capital all contributed to the incomplete transmission. The causes were multiple. But the distributional outcome — cheap liquidity flowing toward asset price inflation and high-margin consumer debt rather than productive investment and affordable mortgage credit — aligned precisely with the incentive structures of the institutions through which the policy had to operate. And those incentive structures were precisely what a more robust regulatory framework, one less shaped over decades by the political relationships described above, might have altered.

The policy was made. Its implementation was systematically deflected. No conspiracy was required — only a structured environment in which the interests of the transmission mechanism and the goals of the policy it was transmitting pointed in different directions.

The Sedimentation

What makes the dynamic described above more than a static problem is that it is a self-reinforcing one. Each cycle does not merely reproduce the existing distribution of political and economic power — it extends and consolidates it, leaving the structural capture progressively more complete and the alternatives progressively more marginal.

The mechanism is straightforward once named. Policy outcomes that reflect the preferences of concentrated economic interests tend, over time, to increase the relative economic weight of those interests. Greater economic weight translates, in the next cycle, into greater political access and more resources to deploy in cultivating it. Greater political access produces outcomes that further extend the advantage. The feedback does not dampen — it amplifies. And it does so across multiple domains simultaneously: in taxation, where the long-run drift has consistently favoured returns on capital over returns on labour; in housing policy, where the interests of existing property owners have systematically outweighed those of prospective ones; in financial regulation, where the post-crisis settlement we have just described left the structural concentration of the sector broadly intact; and in the labour market, where the managed decline of collective bargaining has progressively shifted the distribution of productivity gains toward capital.

The post-crisis period illustrates the sedimentation effect with unusual clarity. The combination of near-zero interest rates, quantitative easing, and the uneven distribution of cheap credit that resulted produced, according to data cited in parliamentary evidence on monetary policy effectiveness, a pattern in which all of the £2.7 trillion rise in UK household wealth between 2007 and 2014 accrued to those over 45, with two thirds going to those over 65, while those aged 16 to 34 saw their wealth decline by around 10%. This was not a random distributional outcome. It was the predictable result of a policy whose transmission was shaped by institutions with strong incentives to direct cheap credit toward asset price inflation — and asset price inflation benefits, overwhelmingly, those who already hold assets.

There is a further dimension to the sedimentation that is rarely named. The pool from which political actors are drawn is itself shaped by the underlying distribution of economic power, which the feedback loop is continuously and directionally shifting. Each cycle in which policy reflects elite preferences over broader ones slightly narrows the social and economic range from which the next generation of political actors is plausibly drawn, slightly deepens the networks through which advancement flows, slightly raises the costs of entry for those who do not begin with proximity to those networks. The selection pressure does not operate only within a given political generation. It shapes the conditions under which the next one forms.

This is not a crisis. It does not announce itself. It accumulates — until the gap between what democratic institutions are nominally supposed to do and what they are structurally capable of doing becomes wide enough to see.

What This Does Not Primarily Depend On

Before proceeding, it is worth being precise about what the argument so far has not assumed. It has not primarily depended on any individual actor behaving with corrupt intent. It has not assumed that interest groups are deliberately seeking to subvert democracy rather than simply pursuing their interests through the mechanisms available to them. The mechanism can operate — and in the main does operate — through entirely ordinary human behaviour: the cultivation of useful relationships, the maintenance of loyalties, the natural drift of perspective toward the views of those one spends time with and depends upon.

It operates, moreover, through the legal exercise of rights that are in most liberal democracies considered fundamental — the right to organise politically, to spend money in pursuit of political goals, to advocate for your interests through the institutions of democratic life. The financial executive who genuinely believes in the policy positions they advocate is not lying. The politician who genuinely respects the expertise of those who have backed their career is not performing. The think tank analyst who produces research conclusions that happen to align with their funders' interests may be entirely sincere. The system produces its outcomes through the aggregation of individually defensible choices made within a structured environment that selects for certain kinds of person, certain kinds of relationship, and certain kinds of outcome.

This is the point that must be held clearly as the analysis proceeds further. We are not describing a system that has been corrupted. We are describing a system that is producing, through its normal operation, outcomes systematically biased toward existing concentrations of power — and that is, in doing so, undermining the regulatory and corrective capacity that we need it to exercise.

But structural problems are not destiny. The selection pressures described here define the field within which political actors operate — they constrain the menu of viable options, they make certain paths easier and others harder, they shape who arrives at the point of decision and with what prior commitments. They do not fully determine which option is taken in any given moment. Within the structured field, choices are real. Institutions can be built that attenuate the pressure. Rules can be changed. Political intelligence, applied with understanding of the actual structural forces at work, can identify where within the possibility space the better outcomes lie and work persistently toward them. The case for doing so is not that it is guaranteed to succeed. It is that the alternative — allowing the sedimentation to continue until the gap between democratic form and democratic substance becomes unbridgeable — is a failure we cannot afford.

The shadows, when we turn to them in the analysis that follows, are darker for what the light already shows. A political system compromised in the ways described here — legally, normally, through the entirely ordinary operation of its own machinery — is one whose structural vulnerabilities are available for exploitation by those willing to go further. Understanding the foundation is the precondition for understanding what has been built upon it.
