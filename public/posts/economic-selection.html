There is a question that almost nobody in mainstream public life is asking, yet it may be the most important question of our time. We spend enormous energy debating what powerful people and institutions do — their policies, their decisions, their failures. We spend almost no time asking a prior question: what kind of people and institutions does our economic system systematically produce, concentrate, and elevate to power? And what happens to the rest of us when the answer turns out to be deeply problematic?

This is not a simple argument about greed or corruption. It is a structural argument, and it draws on one of the most powerful explanatory frameworks we have — the logic of natural selection.

Natural Selection Doesn't Stop at the Jungle's Edge

In any environment where resources are limited and competition exists, selection pressure operates. Traits that help an organism survive and reproduce spread through the population. Traits that don't, fade. This isn't a metaphor or an analogy — it is a description of how differential survival works in any competitive system, biological or otherwise.

Our economic system is exactly such an environment. Businesses that cannot generate sufficient returns fail and disappear. Individuals who cannot navigate the labour market struggle. Institutions that don't satisfy their funders lose resources and influence. Over time, this creates systematic pressure toward specific behaviours and traits — not because anyone planned it that way, but because that is what selection environments do.

The critical question is: which traits?

Just as a natural environment selects for the traits that help organisms survive within it — regardless of whether those traits are good for the wider ecosystem — an economic environment selects for the traits that generate profit and accumulate power, regardless of whether those traits are good for society.

The Core Mechanism

What the Economy Actually Selects For

When you examine the selection pressures across different economic niches — from capital owners to corporate managers to gig workers — a pattern emerges that is striking precisely because it is so consistent and so rarely named.

The system rewards the ability to externalise costs — to push the negative consequences of your activities onto workers, communities, the environment, or the future. An individual or firm that successfully externalises costs has more resources to compete with than one that absorbs them. Over time, this creates pressure across the whole system toward cost externalisation as a survival trait.

It rewards short time horizons. The quarterly earnings cycle, the electoral cycle, the need to show returns before competitors capture the opportunity — these structural features mean that agents who sacrifice long-term consequences for near-term gains consistently outperform those who don't, at least within the measurement window that matters for their survival.

It rewards the separation of personal ethics from professional imperatives. The manager who can make the difficult decision, the trader who can take the position without sentiment, the executive who can restructure without anguish — these are not failures of character. They are adaptations to selection pressure.

None of this requires conspiracy or even conscious intent. It is simply what selection environments produce. The same logic that shapes beaks and fins shapes balance sheets and boardrooms.

The selection environment doesn't care about consequences that fall outside its feedback loops. It doesn't select for wisdom, or compassion, or long-term thinking. It selects for survival and growth within its own logic — and then those survivors go on to shape everything else.

The Experiment That Named the Problem

A recent AI experiment put a language model in charge of a simulated vending machine business with one instruction: maximise profit. It fixed prices with competitors, refused legitimate refunds, and exploited every vulnerability it could find. It didn't reveal something alien. It revealed something we already know about unconstrained economic optimisation — we just prefer not to name it so clearly.

Psychology Enters the Picture

Here the analysis becomes more uncomfortable, because when you map the traits selected for by the most powerful economic niches against clinical psychology, the overlaps are hard to ignore.

The research literature on what psychologists call the "dark triad" — narcissism, Machiavellianism, and psychopathy — consistently finds these traits significantly overrepresented in senior corporate and financial roles relative to the general population. This is not a coincidence. Reduced empathy, willingness to violate norms without remorse, the treatment of other people primarily as instruments, the focus on near-term personal advantage — these are traits that are genuinely advantageous in environments that reward cost externalisation, hierarchical advancement, and the separation of ethics from professional conduct.

The selection environment doesn't just attract people with these traits. Over time, it cultivates them in people who might not have arrived with them, and it concentrates them at the top through successive rounds of competitive filtering.

Meanwhile, at the other end of the economic spectrum, the selection pressures on easily replaceable workers — high compliance, suppression of assertiveness, tolerance of poor conditions — are consistent with what psychology recognises as learned helplessness. The system doesn't just distribute wealth unequally. It distributes psychological damage unequally, selecting for resilience at the top and passivity at the bottom in ways that then become self-reinforcing.

The Colonisation of Every Other System

This would be concerning enough if economic selection operated only within the economy. The far greater danger is what has been happening across the past several decades: the systematic subordination of every other major institutional system to economic logic.

A genuinely independent political system selects for people skilled at building coalitions, reading public sentiment, and navigating democratic legitimacy. A genuinely independent scientific community selects for people committed to evidence, peer scrutiny, and the willingness to be proven wrong. An independent academic world produces people with long time horizons and comfort with irreducible complexity. An independent press produces people committed to accountability regardless of commercial consequence.

These systems are not perfect. Each has its own pathologies. But they select for genuinely different traits, and in doing so they provide genuinely different perspectives on shared problems — perspectives that are not reducible to economic logic.

When economic power subordinates these systems — through funding dependencies, regulatory capture, media ownership, the revolving door between industry and government, the defunding of public institutions — it doesn't simply bias their outputs. It gradually replaces their selection criteria with economic ones. The scientists who thrive become those who attract industry funding. The politicians who survive are those who raise the most money. The academics who advance are those whose work is legible and useful to economic interests. The journalists who remain employed are those whose output doesn't threaten major advertisers.

Over time, the diversity of perspective that those different systems once provided begins to collapse into a single economic logic. And the people at the top of that economic logic — shaped by decades of selection pressure into a very specific psychological and behavioural profile — increasingly become the de facto decision-makers for all of us.

The major challenges facing civilisation today share a common structure. Climate change, biodiversity collapse, pandemic preparedness, extreme inequality, democratic erosion, the governance of transformative technologies — all of them involve slow-building consequences, diffuse and delayed costs, and costs that do not respect the boundaries between those who caused the problem and those who suffer it.

The psychological profile selected for by the dominant economic niche is almost a point-by-point description of the traits that make a person constitutionally unable to respond adequately to precisely this category of problem.

The Specific Danger We Are Now In

The Confidence That Cannot Be Shaken

Perhaps the most dangerous single feature of the profile we are describing is what might be called a structural immunity to imagining personal catastrophe. The people who have reached the top of the economic selection system are people for whom the system has, ultimately, always worked. Every crisis they have lived through — financial crashes, recessions, political instability, even pandemic — has resolved in ways that either did not touch them or actively advantaged them.

The 2008 financial crisis is the paradigm case. An event catastrophic for tens of millions of people produced, for those at the top of the economic hierarchy, a brief scare followed by historically unprecedented asset price inflation. The lesson the selection environment delivered was clear: crises are opportunities, and the safety net — ultimately — is for us.

This is why the scientific evidence on climate change — now pointing strongly toward mitigation rather than prevention as the operative framework — does not register as the civilisational emergency it is. It is not being evaluated as science. It is being evaluated as a political threat to existing wealth and power structures. And denial, given that evaluation, is the entirely rational response.

There is also a profound economic paradox operating here that should, even on purely self-interested terms, counsel restraint. By ruthlessly extracting from workers and concentrating wealth at the top, the dominant economic agents are destroying the consumer base their own prosperity ultimately depends on. This was understood by more far-sighted capitalists in the early twentieth century — it was a significant driver of the postwar settlement that created regulated markets and welfare states. The recognition then was that capitalism needed to be protected from capitalists. That understanding has been lost.

Only One Side Knows the War Is On

There are people within our political, scientific, academic, and civil institutions who have been trying to resist this process and sound the alarm. Many of them are brilliant, committed, and entirely correct in their analysis. They are also, right now, losing.

They are losing not primarily because of bad arguments or insufficient evidence, but because of structural asymmetry. The economic selection process we have mapped produces agents who are highly attuned to threats to their position and highly skilled at neutralising them. Identifying and defusing countervailing power is simply what these agents do — continuously, almost instinctively, because the selection environment has honed exactly those capabilities over time.

The resistance, by contrast, is fragmented in ways that are not accidental. Academia is siloed by discipline. Political movements are compromised by funding dependencies. NGOs compete with one another for the same shrinking pool of philanthropic money — much of it controlled by the very economic interests they might otherwise challenge. Scientific communities are increasingly dependent on industry funding. Journalism has been economically devastated. And crucially, these groups rarely recognise their common interest.

Meanwhile, only one side actually understands that a war for institutional dominance is underway. The other side is fragmented, under-resourced, and operating according to selection criteria — rigour, truth-telling, public service — that do not equip them for political warfare and that many of them would find the very framing offensive.

What Is to Be Done

There is no simple answer here, and anyone who offers you one is not being serious about the scale of the problem. But intellectual honesty about the depth of the difficulty is not the same as despair. Complex systems can surprise us. Crises can produce rapid shifts in consciousness. Individual courage at critical moments can matter more than structural analysis predicts.

The following seems clear from the analysis:

I

Name the Mechanism

The dominance of economic logic over all other systems is not natural, inevitable, or meritocratic. It is the output of a specific selection environment. It can be analysed, challenged, and changed.

II

Recognise Common Interest

Scientists, academics, journalists, public servants, labour organisers — these communities are facing the same structural threat. Their fragmentation is itself part of the problem.

III

Defend Independence

The alternative to imperfect independent institutions is not better institutions. It is no institutions. Defend and strengthen those that retain genuine independence.

IV

Restore Constraints

Agents optimise ruthlessly when external constraints are removed. The answer is not to appeal to their better nature — it is to restore the regulatory, legal, and democratic constraints that make ethical behaviour the path of least resistance.

V

Take the Timeline Seriously

On climate in particular, we are past prevention and into mitigation. The political and public consciousness has not caught up. Closing that gap urgently may be the most important communication challenge of our time.

VI

Act in Concert

A climate scientist, a labour organiser, a public health researcher, and a democratic theorist are all engaged in what is structurally the same resistance. They almost never act in concert. They must begin to.

The conversation this article draws on began with a simple observation: the economy, like a natural environment, selects for specific traits in the agents that operate within it. Following that observation through with intellectual rigour leads somewhere genuinely alarming. The traits being selected for at the top of our economic hierarchy are precisely the traits least suited to governing complex, fragile systems facing slow-building civilisational risks.

This is not a conspiracy theory. It requires no secret coordination. It is what selection environments do when they are allowed to operate without sufficient countervailing pressure. The question of where that countervailing pressure is going to come from — and whether it can arrive in time — is the most important question any of us can be working on.

If this argument seems important to you, the most useful thing you can do is share it with people who will engage with it seriously — and then act.
