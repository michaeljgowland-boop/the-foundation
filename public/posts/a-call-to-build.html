This is a call to help build something that does not yet fully exist. It is written in that spirit — not as a pronouncement from an institution with established authority, but as an argument from a position of honest incompleteness. The Foundation has a thesis, a body of analysis, and a small number of collaborators. What it does not yet have is the breadth of participation that would make it equal to the problem it is trying to address. This document is an attempt to explain why that problem is real, why the response we are proposing is the right kind of response, and why the people we most need to reach have more at stake in this than they may yet recognise.

The Nature of the Crisis

We are living through several crises simultaneously. The climate crisis. The crisis of democratic institutions. The crisis of AI governance. The accelerating concentration of wealth and the political instability it generates. The erosion of the international order that has, for all its imperfections, constrained the worst impulses of states and prevented the largest-scale violence for eight decades. These crises are discussed, analysed, and resisted largely in isolation from one another — by different communities, using different frameworks, in different institutional settings, competing for the same limited pools of attention and resource.

This fragmentation is not an accident. It reflects something real about the nature of the problems: they are genuinely distinct, they require different kinds of expertise, and no single person or institution can hold all of them in focus simultaneously. But the fragmentation is also a strategic failure, because these crises are not independent of one another. They share a common source. And a response that addresses any one of them in isolation, without understanding that common source, will be insufficient — not because it is wrong about its specific target, but because the mechanism producing the crisis will continue to operate unaddressed.

The common source is a failure of collective decision-making. Specifically: a failure in the relationship between the two great decision-making systems that human civilisation has developed — the economic system and the political system — and in the capacity of either, or both together, to process the information most relevant to our survival and flourishing.

Two Systems, One Failure

The market economy is one of the most powerful information-processing systems ever devised. Friedrich Hayek identified its core function clearly in his 1945 essay The Use of Knowledge in Society: the price mechanism aggregates dispersed, local, tacit knowledge that no central planner could collect or process, and coordinates the decisions of millions of independent actors without requiring them to share a common plan or even a common understanding of the whole. This is a genuine achievement. The productive capacity of market economies, and their superiority over centrally planned alternatives in generating material welfare, rests on this informational foundation.

But the market processes only what it can price. And the most consequential facts about our collective situation — the long-term costs of carbon emissions, the systemic risks generated by financial concentration, the value of ecosystems and democratic institutions and social trust, the interests of future generations — are not priced, and cannot be priced without deliberate political intervention. This is not a peripheral limitation. It is a structural feature of the system's architecture. The market's extraordinary processing power is real, but it operates on a systematically incomplete dataset. The information it cannot encode does not disappear; it accumulates as unprocessed risk.

The political system exists, in part, precisely to process what the market cannot. Regulation, taxation, public investment, international agreements — these are mechanisms for encoding into the decision-making system the information that price signals omit. A functioning relationship between the economic and political systems is not an optional enhancement to market capitalism. It is the condition under which market capitalism can avoid destroying the social and ecological foundations on which it depends.

The market is a genuinely powerful decision-making system operating on a systematically incomplete dataset. The political system exists to supply the missing information. The central failure of our time is that the political system is losing the capacity to do so — not by accident, but as the result of structural forces that are now operating without effective constraint.

The structural diagnosis

The relationship between these two systems is not currently in balance. Over the course of the last half-century, and with accelerating force in the last two decades, the economic system has been winning a war of attrition against the political. This has happened through regulatory capture, through the concentration of media ownership, through the transformation of political funding into a market for policy outcomes, and through the systematic elevation — by the selection pressures of market competition — of individuals skilled at short-term extraction rather than long-term governance. The political system has not been corrupted from outside by an alien force. It has been colonised from within by the logic of the system it was supposed to govern.

The Keynesian Moment and Its Unacknowledged Failure

This is not the first time the structural limitations of market economies have been clearly identified. John Maynard Keynes provided, in the middle of the twentieth century, the most rigorous account of why market economies fail to self-correct at the aggregate level, and the most coherent framework for what a corrective political intervention requires. The postwar settlement — Bretton Woods, the welfare state, managed capitalism — was built on his analysis, and it worked. For nearly three decades after 1945, the developed economies achieved rates of growth, compression of inequality, and levels of social stability that had no precedent in the history of industrial capitalism.

Then, in the 1970s, it appeared to stop working. Stagflation — simultaneous high inflation and high unemployment, which Keynesian models struggled to explain — provided the political opening for a monetarist counter-revolution. The intellectual defeat of Keynesianism was accepted on terms that served the interests of those who wanted the political constraints on capital removed. Markets, it was argued, self-correct if left alone. Government intervention distorts the price signals that markets need to function. The Keynesian apparatus was not merely ineffective; it was the problem.

This diagnosis has never been properly examined, let alone refuted. And the reason, we argue, is that the correct diagnosis leads to a conclusion that was politically inconceivable to those doing the analysis: Keynes was not substantially wrong. His framework failed for a reason that his own theory perfectly explains, and that the monetarist framework cannot even see.

Keynes understood that effective macro-economic intervention requires a sovereign actor with the authority and capacity to act unilaterally across an entire market. This jurisdictional coextensiveness — the condition that the governing agent's reach matches the market it is governing — was the silent structural assumption of the whole Keynesian framework. The postwar Bretton Woods system preserved it deliberately: Keynes himself designed the capital controls that prevented the international mobility of finance from eroding national policy space. His famous insistence that "finance must be primarily national" was not a parochial prejudice. It was a structural requirement.

What happened in the 1970s was not the failure of Keynesian theory. It was the collapse of the international architecture that made Keynesian policy possible. The breakdown of Bretton Woods in 1971, and the subsequent decades of capital account liberalisation, progressively destroyed the jurisdictional coextensiveness that effective intervention requires. By the time Thatcher and Reagan were dismantling the Keynesian policy apparatus, they were in large part dismantling something that had already been made unworkable — not by its internal contradictions, but by the globalisation of capital that its architects had specifically designed to prevent.

The monetarist alternative was intellectually convenient precisely because it did not require this conclusion to be faced. If markets self-correct, the awkward politics of building supranational governance capacity can be avoided. The theory fitted the political constraints of the moment. That is always a warning sign about a theory.

The Keynesian Insight

Markets fail to self-correct at the aggregate level. Effective intervention requires a sovereign actor whose jurisdictional reach is coextensive with the market it governs. This was the silent structural assumption of the entire postwar settlement.

II

The Real Failure

Bretton Woods preserved the national policy space Keynesian intervention requires. Its collapse, and the subsequent liberalisation of capital movement, destroyed the jurisdictional coextensiveness that made the framework work. The theory did not fail. Its institutional preconditions were removed.

III

The Unacknowledged Consequence

The correct conclusion — that governance capacity must be rebuilt at the scale of actually-existing global markets — was politically inconceivable. The monetarist alternative was accepted not because it was correct but because it was convenient. We are living with the consequences of that choice.

Dani Rodrik of Harvard's Kennedy School has formalised this as a trilemma: deep economic integration, national sovereignty, and democratic governance cannot coexist simultaneously. The postwar order chose to limit integration to preserve the other two. The neoliberal turn chose deep integration and sacrificed the others. There is no fourth option that avoids this trade-off. The only paths forward are a deliberate limiting of economic integration — which would require reversing decades of globalisation against the resistance of those who have benefited from it — or the construction of genuine governance capacity at the scale of global markets. The Foundation's analysis points firmly toward the second.

The Scale of Governance Required

The conclusion that follows from this analysis is demanding, and we state it without softening: the governance capacity required to correct for the structural failures of global markets cannot be built within any single national jurisdiction. Not in the largest democracy, not in the most powerful economy, not in any state that exists today. The market has outgrown every sovereign that might govern it. The missing information — the externalities, the long-term systemic risks, the commons, the interests of future generations — accumulates not because governments lack the will to encode it, though sometimes they do, but because the encoding, if done by one jurisdiction, is immediately arbitraged away by the others.

This is why the standard progressive prescription — elect better governments, pass stronger regulations, tax the rich more — is necessary but insufficient. It is not wrong. Better governments, stronger regulations, and fairer taxation are all genuinely important. But they are being attempted within a jurisdictional architecture that structurally limits their effectiveness, and without an honest account of that limitation, even successful campaigns at the national level will find their gains progressively eroded by the global mobility of the capital they are trying to govern.

What is required is the construction of multilateral governance frameworks with real authority — frameworks capable of setting the terms on which access to major markets is granted, of encoding the missing information into the international trading and financial system, of making the cost of externalising onto the global commons visible and real to every actor within the system. This is a large ambition. It is also the minimum ambition consistent with addressing the problem as it actually exists.

The Selection Problem

This analysis has a corollary that is uncomfortable but essential to state clearly. The people who have risen to positions of greatest power and wealth within the current system have done so, in large part, by being exceptionally good at what the current system rewards: the extraction of short-term value, the externalisation of costs, the capture of regulatory and political institutions. These are not character flaws in any simple sense. They are the adaptive responses of capable people to the incentive structures they operated within. The system selected for these qualities because these qualities generated competitive advantage within it.

But the qualities that generate competitive advantage within a system are not the same as the qualities required to govern that system well — particularly when the system is generating civilisational-scale risks that it cannot itself perceive. The people best positioned to act are, in significant measure, the people whose success has been built on the blind spots that need to be corrected.

We do not say this as criticism. We say it as a structural observation with a direct implication for this argument: the people most resistant to the regulation and redistribution that a functioning decision-making architecture requires are not irrational. They are responding rationally to a mistaken model of where their interests lie. The threat to their wealth and security is not regulation. The threat is the continued failure of the systems that make sustained wealth and security possible — the ecological systems, the institutional systems, the social systems that unregulated global market competition is currently destroying faster than political institutions can defend them.

Peter Turchin's quantitative historical analysis, developed across a series of works including Ages of Discord (2023), demonstrates with uncomfortable empirical force that periods of extreme elite wealth concentration are reliably followed by institutional breakdown, political instability, and the destruction of the conditions of prosperity — including elite prosperity. The historical record does not support the proposition that concentrated wealth is self-sustaining. It supports the proposition that concentrated wealth, unmediated by functional political institutions, generates the conditions of its own destruction.

The Great Powers and the Only Viable Path

The same structural logic applies to the great powers whose behaviour is most consequential for any global governance architecture. The United States, China, Russia, India — no solution that excludes them is a solution. No external pressure, however well-intentioned, can coerce actors of this scale into compliance with a framework they have not chosen. The history of attempted external regime change against major powers is a history of failure, frequently of catastrophic failure, and the Foundation has no interest in adding to it.

This is not moral indifference to the policies of authoritarian states. It is clarity about what is possible and what is not. The argument for a functioning global governance architecture has to be made to Beijing and Moscow and Washington in the same terms as it is made to those who already accept the liberal internationalist framework: here is why the system that is currently working for you is generating the conditions of its own failure, and here is what your genuine long-term interests require.

This argument is not without foundation. China is simultaneously the world's largest carbon emitter and among the countries most exposed to certain categories of climate risk — coastal flooding, water stress, agricultural disruption at scale. Its leadership is not ignorant of this. The gap between stated commitments and actual behaviour is not primarily a product of indifference; it is a product of the collective action problem that the absence of a functioning global governance architecture creates. If one major power bears the costs of decarbonisation while others do not, it accepts competitive disadvantage for a collective benefit it cannot capture unilaterally. This is precisely the problem that multilateral governance frameworks are designed to solve — and it applies to every major power, including the ones most resistant to external pressure on other grounds.

The postwar international order — Bretton Woods, the UN system, the Marshall Plan — was constructed by powers with profoundly different interests, some with recent histories of violent conflict, because enough of their leaders understood that the alternative to institutional construction was a repetition of the catastrophe they had just lived through. It was imperfect, partial, and shaped by the power distributions of the moment. But it worked well enough, for long enough, to produce the most sustained period of great-power peace in modern history. We are not starting from nothing. We are arguing for the reconstruction and extension of something that has worked before, under conditions that were in some respects more difficult than our own.

The Question of How to Govern

Reconstructing governance capacity at the scale of global markets is necessary. But it is not sufficient, and here the Foundation's analysis parts company with a strand of progressive thinking that it is important to address directly.

Keynes himself was clear that not all political interventions are equal. Democratic systems create incentives for politicians to promise what cannot be delivered — stimulus without inflation, protection without inefficiency, growth without environmental cost. The electoral cycle is structurally short-term in ways that compound, rather than correct for, the short-termism of markets. Simply reasserting political control over economic decisions, without addressing the incentive structures that shape political decisions, risks substituting one form of short-termism for another.

The model that has worked most durably — and that needs to be extended, not merely repeated — is the relationship between central banks and democratic governments that emerged from the late twentieth century. Monetary policy objectives are set democratically: price stability, employment, financial stability are political choices about what we value. But the operation of monetary policy — the setting of interest rates, the management of the money supply, the regulation of financial institutions — is insulated from the electoral cycle and delegated to technically expert bodies, operating transparently within democratically mandated objectives, accountable for outcomes but not subject to day-to-day political direction.

The encoding of missing information into the economic system — the social cost of carbon, the systemic risk premium on financial concentration, the long-term value of ecosystems and institutions — is a technical problem of comparable complexity to monetary policy, and it requires a comparable institutional response. Not elected politicians deciding what the social cost of carbon is in the run-up to an election, but bodies constituted from the relevant scientific and economic disciplines — climate scientists, ecologists, economists, epidemiologists, systems theorists — operating within democratically determined objectives, charged with the technical assessment of long-term costs and benefits and the mechanisms best suited to encoding them.

This is not a proposal to remove democratic control over ends. It is a proposal to insulate the technical determination of means from the electoral distortions that currently prevent those means from being determined correctly. The distinction matters. What kind of world do we want, what trade-offs between present and future are acceptable, whose interests count and how — these are irreducibly political questions that democratic processes are the right mechanism to answer. How best to achieve those goals, what the actual long-term costs of different courses of action are, what the evidence from climate science and economics and ecology shows about the consequences of our choices — these are technical questions, and submitting them to majoritarian politics produces systematically worse answers than delegating them to expert bodies with clear mandates and genuine independence.

Democratic Money and the Banking System

Alongside globalisation, one of the most significant and least discussed structural sources of both inequality and monetary policy failure is the architecture of the modern banking system — specifically, the privileged access that private financial institutions have to central bank facilities.

Central banks create money and lend it to commercial banks at the base rate. Commercial banks on-lend to businesses and households at higher rates. The spread between the base rate and commercial lending rates represents a structural subsidy to the private banking sector — preferential access to the cheapest money in the system, which is not available to anyone else. This is not a market outcome. It is an institutional choice, and it has profound distributional consequences.

The consequences were made visible, with unusual clarity, by the response to the 2008 financial crisis. Quantitative easing created enormous quantities of central bank money, which flowed to commercial banks and from there into asset markets rather than into productive investment or household income. The monetary transmission mechanism — the chain by which central bank policy is supposed to affect the real economy — was broken at precisely the point of private bank intermediation. Banks used cheap money to repair their balance sheets and inflate asset prices. Those who held assets became dramatically wealthier. Those who did not were largely unaffected by the policy nominally intended to help them. The result was one of the largest single transfers of relative wealth in peacetime history, conducted through a mechanism that most of its beneficiaries did not understand and most of its victims could not see.

The structural question this raises is: why should access to the central bank — an institution created by democratic societies to serve the public interest — be restricted to licensed private financial intermediaries? The case for universal central bank access is not primarily redistributive, though its redistributive consequences would be significant. It is a case about the integrity of monetary policy itself. Direct accounts at the central bank, accessible to all citizens and firms, would restore the monetary transmission mechanism that private bank intermediation currently blocks. Counter-cyclical policy could operate directly, without depending on the willingness of commercial banks to pass on its effects. The structural subsidy to private finance — and with it one of the major institutional engines of wealth concentration — would be substantially reduced.

This proposal is no longer at the fringes of economic thinking. The development of central bank digital currencies has opened serious institutional debate about the relationship between central banks and the public they serve. Adair Turner, former chair of the UK Financial Services Authority, argued in Between Debt and the Devil (2015) that the dominance of private bank money creation is a structural source of financial instability as well as inequality. The Bank for International Settlements has published substantive work on the implications of universal central bank access. The question is not whether this is technically feasible. It is whether the political will to pursue it can be constructed — which returns us directly to the coordination problem the Foundation exists to address.

Why Fragmented Responses Are Insufficient

There are already many people doing important work on every dimension of the crisis we have described. Climate scientists have established the physical basis of the ecological emergency beyond reasonable doubt. Democratic theorists and civil society organisations are fighting to defend specific institutional structures against specific threats. AI safety researchers are grappling seriously with the governance challenges of transformative technology. Economists working on inequality, tax justice, and corporate governance are developing the analytical tools needed to understand what a rebalanced decision-making architecture might look like. International relations scholars are mapping the constraints on multilateral cooperation with increasing precision.

This work is not wrong. It does not need to be replaced or redirected. It needs something it currently lacks: a common framework that connects it, and a forum in which the people doing it can develop a more coherent collective strategy than any of them can develop alone.

The reason coherence matters here is not aesthetic. It is strategic. The forces driving the crisis operate through a single dominant logic — the logic of short-term market competition operating without adequate governance — that is simultaneously shaping outcomes in every domain. A response that addresses climate policy without addressing the institutional capture that prevents implementation; that defends democratic institutions without addressing the economic concentration funding their subversion; that works on AI governance without addressing the selection pressures determining who controls AI development; that advocates Keynesian-style intervention without addressing the jurisdictional mismatch that makes national intervention ineffective — such a response will find itself running uphill, spending its energy against consequences rather than causes.

Coherence cannot be imposed. It cannot be decreed by a coordinating body or produced by a manifesto, including this one. It can only emerge from genuine intellectual and strategic contact between people who understand different parts of the problem clearly — contact that creates, over time, a shared map precise enough to support coordinated action without requiring uniformity of approach or the subordination of specific expertise to a general programme.

This is what the Foundation is attempting to create. Not a movement with a fixed plan, but a space in which the plan can be developed by the people best equipped to develop it — across disciplines, across sectors, and across the geopolitical divisions that any viable solution must ultimately bridge.

The Opposition This Will Face

A document that proposes the reconstruction of global governance capacity, the democratisation of central bank access, and the encoding of long-term systemic costs into the price mechanism — without acknowledging that these proposals threaten powerful interests that will resist them — would not deserve to be taken seriously. We have no intention of being that naive, and we do not intend to waste the time of people serious enough to have read this far.

The Foundation's agenda, taken as a whole, is manageable to most of the interests it disturbs. The case to the super-rich, as we have argued, can be made on rational grounds: the system that has rewarded them is generating the conditions of its own failure, and the governance architecture we are describing is more likely to preserve what actually matters about their prosperity than the alternative. They may resist that argument — habit, ideology, and the short-term logic of competitive position are powerful forces — but the argument is reachable. The case to authoritarian great powers is harder, but it too rests on genuine shared interests that can be argued to honestly.

There is one constituency for whom the argument is structurally different, and we should be direct about it. The major private financial institutions whose business model depends on privileged access to central bank facilities are not facing a proposal to regulate them more stringently or tax them more heavily. They are facing a proposal to remove the structural basis of their competitive advantage entirely. Universal central bank access, if implemented, would eliminate the spread between base rate and commercial lending rates that currently represents an institutionalised subsidy — not a market outcome, but a political choice, embedded so deeply in the architecture of modern finance that it has come to seem natural. There is no version of this reform that leaves their current position intact. The threat, from their perspective, is existential to their business model, and they will recognise it as such.

Their means of response are formidable and, by the standards of most political actors, remarkably unconstrained. The revolving door between major financial institutions and the regulatory, treasury, and central banking bodies that govern them is one of the most thoroughly documented features of modern political economy. Simon Johnson, former chief economist of the IMF, described the United States version of this capture with unusual candour in his 2010 essay The Quiet Coup — the title refers to the financial sector's effective takeover of economic policymaking, accomplished not through corruption in any simple sense but through the intellectual capture of the institutions and individuals making the decisions. The UK equivalent, the relationship between the City of London and the Treasury, is older and in some respects more deeply embedded.

Beyond direct political capture, the financial sector shapes intellectual debate through the funding of think tanks, research institutes, and academic centres whose output systematically favours the sector's interests while presenting itself as independent analysis. It shapes media coverage through access journalism and through the structural dependence of financial reporting on the goodwill of the institutions it covers. And it deploys, with considerable sophistication, the genuine complexity of its operations as a political weapon — making the case that any reform of the banking system will trigger runs, destroy credit creation, and produce economic chaos, in ways that make it genuinely difficult for non-specialist audiences to distinguish legitimate technical objection from self-interested obfuscation.

The Foundation is not unaware of what it is proposing. A project that threatens the structural interests of the most politically powerful sector in the developed economies will be opposed with resources and sophistication that dwarf anything we can currently deploy. We say this not to discourage, but because the only honest response to that reality is to build something that cannot be neutralised by any single line of attack.

The strategic reality

This is precisely why the breadth and intellectual seriousness of the coalition matters as a strategic question, not merely an organisational one. The financial sector cannot simultaneously capture the climate science community, delegitimise the democratic reform agenda, discredit AI safety concerns, and neutralise the work of economists who have no financial sector funding. The broader and more genuinely multi-domain the Foundation's coalition, the harder it becomes to reduce the entire project to "people who want to abolish banks" — which will be the preferred reductive framing, because it is the one most likely to make the serious parts of the audience stop listening.

The Foundation's best defence is also its best argument: to be exactly what it claims to be. Rigorous, honest, intellectually serious across multiple domains, and genuinely independent of the interests it is analysing. This is harder than it sounds. Funding from sources with a stake in the outcome compromises independence in ways that are often invisible to those involved. The revolving door affects intellectual life as well as regulatory life. The Foundation will need to be deliberate and transparent about the conditions under which it accepts support, and honest about its own interests and limitations in ways that most institutions are not.

We do not pretend this will be easy or that the outcome is assured. We claim only that it is necessary, that the alternative is to leave the field to those who are not constrained by the norms of honest argument, and that the historical record shows that well-organised coalitions with serious intellectual foundations can, under the right conditions and with sufficient patience, shift the terms on which these debates are conducted. The postwar Keynesian settlement, the civil rights movement, the environmental movement — none of these succeeded quickly or easily or without opposition from interests far more powerful than the movements themselves at the outset. All of them changed the rules.

A New Kind of Collective Intelligence

We are living through a transition between modes of production — from the industrial-democratic settlement of the twentieth century to something we do not yet have a settled name for. The social and political superstructures built on the old mode are under strain in ways now visible to everyone. But as the analysis developed in these pages argues at length, transitions of this kind do not have predetermined outcomes. They produce a genuine expansion of possibility — including the possibility of catastrophe, and the possibility of something better than what preceded them.

The transition we are living through has one feature that distinguishes it from all previous ones: the emergence of artificial intelligence as a participant in collective cognition. This can go badly — AI capabilities concentrated in the hands of actors already skilled at using technological advantage to extract value and resist accountability would dramatically accelerate the dynamics we have described. But it can also open possibilities that the existing frameworks for thinking about collective action do not fully capture: new forms of synthesis across disciplinary boundaries, new capacities for processing the complexity of systemic risk, new modes of intellectual collaboration between human and artificial intelligence that are neither the displacement of human judgment nor its simple augmentation, but something more genuinely dialectical.

The Foundation is itself an example of what that might look like in practice. The analysis in these pages has been developed in genuine intellectual collaboration between a human thinker working on these problems for over a decade and an AI system capable of engaging with them seriously. We credit this collaboration openly because honesty about it is part of the argument. One of the pathologies of the current moment is the gap between what institutions and individuals actually believe and what they say publicly — the performance of certainty where there is doubt, the performance of consensus where there is conflict, the performance of neutrality where there are genuine interests at stake. The Foundation cannot credibly call for a new kind of collective intelligence, or for the reconstruction of honest institutional relationships, while concealing the nature of its own intellectual process.

Coherence is not a plan. It is what emerges when people who understand different parts of a problem genuinely engage with each other — and begin to see, across the boundaries of their particular expertise, the shape of the whole. It cannot be imposed from above. It can only be created by building the conditions in which it becomes possible.

The Foundation's operating principle

A Call to Build

The Foundation is not finished. It is, at this moment, a thesis, a body of analysis, and a conviction that the connective work described in this document is both necessary and possible. It is not an institution with resources, staff, and a track record. It is an argument in search of the people who can test it, extend it, and where it is wrong, correct it.

We are looking for people who understand one or more dimensions of the crisis clearly — from within the sciences, the academy, the professions, the worlds of policy and practice and business — and who have arrived, through their own work, at the frustration of finding that their specific expertise is insufficient to the scale of the problem. People who are already doing important work and who recognise that the fragmentation of that work is a strategic liability they have not been able to address alone.

We are looking for people with power and resources who are willing to examine the argument that the system which has rewarded them is generating the conditions of its own failure — and that the rational response to that argument is not resistance to the governance architecture being proposed, but serious engagement with what such an architecture would require and what role they might play in building it.

We are looking for people in every part of the world, including those whose governments are currently most resistant to the multilateral frameworks this analysis requires — because no solution that excludes them is a solution, and because the case for a functioning global decision-making architecture can be made honestly to anyone whose long-term interests depend on civilisational stability. Which is everyone.

We are not offering a finished programme. The details of what needs to be done in any specific domain — climate policy, AI governance, democratic reform, economic restructuring, the reform of international financial institutions — need to be worked out by experts in those domains, not prescribed by a coordinating body with a political philosophy. The Foundation's role is to create the conditions in which that work can happen with greater coherence and mutual support than it currently achieves, and to maintain the intellectual honesty that makes genuine collaboration possible.

The window in which it is possible to act on these problems through the institutions we have — imperfect, strained, and partially captured as they are — is not permanent. Institutional fabric, once destroyed, is not quickly rebuilt. The question is not whether the current arrangements are adequate. It is whether they are worth defending and reforming while that remains possible, and whether enough people are willing to do that work together.

The case for urgency

History does not move by necessity toward better arrangements. But it does move — and the direction it moves depends on whether the people who understand what is happening are willing to act on that understanding, together, while the possibility of acting remains open.

This is that moment. This is the invitation. The Foundation exists to be built — and it cannot be built without you.
