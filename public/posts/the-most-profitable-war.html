In 1933, the United States ended Prohibition. The experiment had run for thirteen years, and its results were not ambiguous. The criminalisation of alcohol had not reduced drinking significantly. It had created a vast and violent criminal market, enriched organised crime on a scale that would define American criminal enterprise for generations, corrupted law enforcement and political institutions across the country, and generated a public health catastrophe as unregulated spirits blinded and killed consumers who had no recourse to quality control or legal protection. The lesson drawn, at the time and since, was clear: criminalise a substance with broad demand and you do not eliminate the demand. You hand its satisfaction to criminal enterprise and let them set the terms.

Within a decade of that lesson being learned, the United States began the process that would lead to the global criminalisation of cannabis, heroin, cocaine, and a widening list of other substances — a process that accelerated through the 1950s and reached its apotheosis in Nixon's declaration of a formal War on Drugs in 1971. The policy has been maintained, extended, and defended ever since, against evidence that has grown from substantial to overwhelming. It has failed by every measure its proponents claimed to care about. Drug use has not been eliminated, or significantly reduced, or even stabilised. The criminal markets it created have grown to become among the most powerful economic and political forces on the planet. The violence, corruption, and public health consequences have fallen most heavily on the poorest and most vulnerable communities in both producing and consuming countries.

The question this paper asks is not whether the War on Drugs has failed. That question has been answered. The question is why it persists — why a policy whose failure is manifest, whose harms are documented in detail, and whose logic was explicitly refuted by a directly analogous historical precedent before it was ever seriously implemented, has been maintained for more than half a century against all contrary evidence. The answer, when examined using the methodological framework established in the preceding piece, tells us something essential about how democratic governance actually works when the structural interests at stake are large enough.

The Coalition That Built the War

The origins of drug criminalisation in the United States are unusually well documented, in part because several of the key actors were sufficiently unguarded, at sufficient distance from events, to say what they were actually doing.

The Federal Bureau of Narcotics, under Harry Anslinger, was the institutional engine of early drug criminalisation. Anslinger had built his career in Prohibition enforcement. As repeal became inevitable in the early 1930s, the Bureau of Narcotics — which Anslinger had headed since its creation in 1930 — faced an obvious institutional threat: the disappearance of the enforcement domain on which its existence and budget depended. The campaign for cannabis criminalisation that Anslinger led through the mid-1930s, culminating in the Marihuana Tax Act of 1937, solved this problem. It was accompanied by congressional testimony that the historical record has consistently shown to be fabricated — invented crime statistics, racial panic, deliberate misrepresentation of the medical and scientific evidence available at the time. This is Tier I: it is in the congressional record and has been documented extensively by historians of drug policy, including academic accounts by Michael Aldrich, Richard Bonnie, and Charles Whitebread in their analysis of the legislative history for the National Commission on Marihuana and Drug Abuse.

The second element of the founding coalition has been stated, with unusual directness, by one of its principal architects. John Ehrlichman served as Nixon's chief domestic policy advisor and was a key figure in the design of the War on Drugs. In 1994, he told journalist Dan Baum — in remarks Baum did not publish until 2016, in Harper's Magazine, following Ehrlichman's death — that the Nixon administration understood precisely what it was doing. The War on Drugs, Ehrlichman said, was designed to attack two of the Nixon administration's political enemies: the anti-war left and Black Americans. By associating the left with marijuana and Black communities with heroin, and then criminalising both heavily, the administration could disrupt those communities, arrest their leaders, and raid their homes — all without ever being forced to say publicly what it was actually targeting. "Did we know we were lying about the drugs?" Ehrlichman asked. "Of course we did." This is Tier I: a direct admission from a primary source, published in a mainstream outlet, subsequently widely reported and never credibly disputed.

The third element is the one that receives least attention in mainstream accounts, and it is the one most important for the argument this series is making. Criminal enterprises — the organised crime networks that had been built on Prohibition-era alcohol trafficking — had the most direct and substantial financial interest in the continuation of drug criminalisation. Criminalisation is what creates the margin. Legal alcohol is cheap. Illegal cocaine is expensive not because it is difficult to produce but because the illegality of every step in its supply chain creates the risk premium that is the source of the profit. The War on Drugs, understood as a market intervention, is the single most important structural support for the profitability of drug trafficking. Without it, the cartels and mafias that have grown to become among the most powerful economic and political actors in the world would not exist in anything like their current form.

That criminal enterprises understood this — and that their understanding translated into political activity aimed at maintaining criminalisation — is a Tier II inference. We cannot point to a documented meeting between Harry Anslinger and the leaders of organised crime at which they agreed to support drug criminalisation in exchange for the profits it would generate. What we can point to is the structural logic: that the interests aligned perfectly, that the political outcomes consistently served those interests, and that the resources available to organised crime for corrupting political processes were, even in this early period, substantial. The Crowley account of a French bishop buying a heroin processing plant before criminalisation was enacted — noted in our discussion as a Tier III hypothesis requiring substantiation — is consistent with this pattern, even if it cannot currently be treated as more than that.

The Intelligence Dimension

The relationship between intelligence agencies and the drug trade is one of the most thoroughly documented and most consistently ignored subjects in the political history of the twentieth century.

The structure of the relationship is not hard to understand. Intelligence agencies operating in complex political environments need resources that cannot appear in official budgets and relationships that cannot appear in official accounts. Criminal networks operating across international borders have both the logistical infrastructure and the financial capacity that covert operations require. The incentives for cooperation are powerful on both sides, and the documentation of that cooperation — once it becomes available, through congressional investigations, declassified documents, and the accounts of participants — is consistent across multiple theatres and multiple decades.

The most extensively documented American case is the Iran-Contra affair and the associated evidence of CIA involvement in cocaine trafficking into the United States. Gary Webb's 1996 investigative series in the San Jose Mercury News, subsequently expanded into the book Dark Alliance, documented the connection between the CIA-backed Contra networks, their Nicaraguan drug trafficking partners, and the crack cocaine epidemic in Los Angeles. The immediate institutional response was to destroy Webb's credibility — a coordinated effort involving major American newspapers that has itself become a case study in the dynamics of investigative journalism and institutional power. The CIA's own inspector general, in a 1998 report, confirmed the core of Webb's findings: that the agency had known about drug trafficking by Contra networks and had neither reported it to the Drug Enforcement Administration nor taken steps to stop it. This is Tier I. Webb's conclusions about the scale and directness of CIA involvement remain more contested — Tier II — but the core finding of agency knowledge and non-intervention is in the official record.

The Lebanese case, while less extensively documented in English-language sources, follows the same structural pattern. During the Lebanese civil war, the CIA's relationships with various Lebanese factions — including Christian Phalangist groups — created the conditions for a deliberate blind eye toward cannabis and heroin trafficking through the Bekaa Valley. The Phalangists' involvement in the drug trade was known to Western intelligence services; the intelligence relationships were considered more valuable than drug enforcement. This is a Tier II inference, supported by the accounts of investigators and journalists who covered the period, including reporting in the Washington Post and subsequent academic work on the political economy of the Lebanese civil war.

The Harlem heroin epidemic of the late 1960s and the question of whether it was actively facilitated as a tool of social control sits in more complex evidential territory. The structural logic is compelling: the Nixon administration had explicitly identified Black political radicalisation as a target, heroin was devastating Black urban communities precisely as that radicalisation was at its height, and the intelligence and law enforcement agencies involved in COINTELPRO — the FBI's programme of surveillance and disruption of Black political organisations — had demonstrated a willingness to use any available tool against those organisations. The trajectory of groups like the Crips and Bloods — beginning as politically conscious community self-defence organisations and ending as street-level drug distribution networks — is consistent with a deliberate disruption strategy, though the evidence for active facilitation rather than strategic neglect remains at Tier III. What is Tier I is that COINTELPRO existed, that it targeted Black political organisations specifically, and that its methods included deliberate disruption and the exploitation of criminal networks. The rest is inference from that established base.

The consistent pattern is not that intelligence agencies built the drug trade. It is that wherever the drug trade intersected with their operational interests, they found reasons not to disrupt it — and sometimes found reasons to protect it. The War on Drugs and the covert use of drug networks were not in tension. They were complementary. The war provided the public rationale for enforcement that fell, as designed, on communities that posed political threats. The covert relationships provided operational resources that could not be officially accounted for.

The structural logic

The Evidence That Was Ignored

The case against drug criminalisation has been made repeatedly, in multiple jurisdictions, through controlled policy experiments whose results were clear enough to constitute something close to proof of concept. The consistent response of the governments with the most to lose from that proof has been to ignore it.

Portugal decriminalised the personal possession of all drugs in 2001 — not legalisation, but the removal of criminal penalties for possession combined with investment in treatment and harm reduction. The results, tracked over subsequent decades by the European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction and analysed in an independent assessment published by the Cato Institute in 2009, were unambiguous across every significant metric. Drug-related HIV infections fell dramatically. Drug-related deaths declined. Drug use rates did not increase and in several categories fell. The burden on the criminal justice system reduced substantially. Portugal is now the most thoroughly documented natural experiment in drug policy reform available, and its findings have been replicated in subsequent academic literature to the point where any serious review of the evidence must engage with them.

Switzerland's heroin-assisted treatment programme, introduced in the 1990s following a public health crisis centred on open drug scenes in Zurich and other cities, produced results sufficiently clear that they were endorsed by national referendum and the programme was subsequently expanded. Among participants, crime fell sharply, health outcomes improved dramatically, and the programme effectively eliminated the street heroin market in the areas where it operated. The evidence base is robust, has been peer-reviewed, and is cited in major public health literature including publications by the World Health Organisation.

In the United Kingdom, a heroin prescription programme in the Wirral and subsequently expanded to other areas showed similar results at the individual level, with patients stabilising, reducing criminal activity, and maintaining employment at rates substantially higher than those on methadone programmes or receiving no treatment. The evidence was available. The political will to extend it was not.

The enclave problem — raised in our preparatory discussion — is worth addressing directly, because it is sometimes used to argue that experiments in harm reduction fail in practice. Where harm reduction programmes exist within a broader environment of criminalisation, they tend to attract users from surrounding areas precisely because they offer services unavailable elsewhere. The resulting concentration of use and dealing in the vicinity of harm reduction facilities is real, and it generates local opposition that is politically exploitable. But this is not evidence that harm reduction fails. It is evidence that partial reform in a hostile policy environment produces specific and predictable distortions. The solution the evidence points toward is comprehensive rather than enclave-based reform — which is precisely what the evidence from Portugal and Switzerland supports, and precisely what has been most consistently resisted.

The Niche and Its Occupants

There is a principle in evolutionary biology — convergent evolution — that describes the independent emergence of similar forms in unrelated lineages responding to similar selective pressures. The ichthyosaur, the dolphin, and the shark are not closely related. They converge on similar body plans because the aquatic predator niche selects for similar solutions regardless of the lineage entering it. The logic is not about genealogy. It is about the structure of the environment and the solutions it rewards.

The same logic operates in economic and political space. Drug criminalisation creates a niche — a demand that exists and will not be eliminated by prohibition, combined with a legal vacuum that removes regulatory competition and inflates the profit margin available to whoever fills the niche. That niche will be filled. The question is not whether but by what, and the answer under criminalisation is: by whatever organisational form is best adapted to operating profitably in a high-risk, high-margin, enforcement-hostile environment. That form is the cartel. The Sinaloa Cartel, the various Italian mafias, the Russian organised crime networks, the Romanian networks — these are not aberrations from a normally functioning market. They are the market's solution to the niche that criminalisation created and has maintained.

The contemporary United Kingdom illustrates the resulting coexistence with particular clarity. Legal medical cannabis is now available — to those who understand how to navigate the private prescription system, can afford the associated costs, and are willing to engage with a bureaucratic process that most people with drug dependencies are poorly positioned to manage. Illegal cannabis remains widely available, cheaper, more accessible, and carrying none of the formal barriers of the legal route. The legal and illegal markets coexist not because regulation has failed but because regulation has been implemented in a form sufficiently restricted that it does not eliminate the niche the illegal market occupies. The gap between legal and illegal availability is maintained by the restrictions. And the restrictions are maintained by a political environment that the illegal market's beneficiaries have every interest in sustaining.

This is the point at which the War on Drugs intersects most directly with the broader argument of this series. The cartels and criminal networks that benefit from criminalisation are not passive beneficiaries of a policy made for other reasons. They are political actors with substantial resources and well-documented histories of deploying those resources to shape the political environments they operate within. The penetration of Mexican political institutions by cartel money is the most visible case — documented extensively by journalists, academics, and official inquiries — but it is not geographically limited. The evidence examined elsewhere in this series shows cartel and organised crime money moving through the same laundering infrastructure that moves Russian intelligence money, into the same financial and political systems in the United Kingdom and United States.

The British Case

The United Kingdom under the Conservative governments of 2010 to 2024 provides an unusually concentrated illustration of the dynamics this paper has described, because the contradictions became sufficiently visible to document.

Those governments maintained, across fourteen years, among the most inflexible positions on drug criminalisation of any comparable democracy. Proposals for drug consumption rooms — supported by public health professionals, by local authorities including the Scottish Government, and by substantial peer-reviewed evidence of effectiveness — were repeatedly blocked. The Glasgow drug consumption room proposal, endorsed by Scottish Government and backed by a strong public health consensus, was vetoed by the Home Office on legal grounds that most legal experts regarded as at least questionable. Drug reform proposals from within the Conservative Party itself were suppressed. The public position was that Britain's drug laws were a matter of public health and moral principle.

The private reality was somewhat different. Systematic drug testing of surfaces in Westminster — conducted by journalists and, on at least one occasion, by the House of Commons authorities themselves — found cocaine residue in multiple parliamentary locations including toilets used by MPs and staff. Reporting on the social behaviour of senior Conservative figures, including a then-Cabinet minister's attendance at events where drug use was reported, was a matter of public record. The people maintaining the strictest public position on criminalisation were, by the available evidence, among its most casual personal violators. This is a Tier I observation based on published reporting; the specific inference that this represents deliberate hypocrisy rather than private weakness is Tier II, but the structural pattern — selective enforcement operating in the same direction as the general asymmetry this series documents — is consistent with every other domain examined.

The deeper connection is established through the Russia Report, examined in detail in the following piece. That report, produced by the Intelligence and Security Committee and suppressed by Downing Street for nine months before being released in redacted form in 2020, establishes — carefully and in the language of intelligence assessment — that laundered money has entered British political life including donations to political parties. The nature of laundered money is definitional: it is money that has been processed to conceal its criminal origin or criminal destination. Drug trafficking is among the primary sources of the funds that flow through the laundering infrastructure the Russia Report describes. The same financial architecture that moves Russian intelligence money moves cartel money. They are not separate systems. They are the same system serving multiple clients.

A government receiving donations routed through that architecture while maintaining strict drug criminalisation is not necessarily — at the level of individual consciousness — aware of the connection. The involuntary incorporation mechanism described in the methodology piece applies here as it does elsewhere: actors can be embedded in structures whose full nature they do not know, and find themselves serving interests they have not explicitly endorsed. But the structural alignment — between the political position on drug policy and the financial interests of those whose money flows through the laundering infrastructure — is consistent, directional, and explicable.

The War on Drugs is the most expensive and most durable policy failure in the democratic world. It has consumed trillions of dollars, imprisoned millions of people, devastated communities across multiple continents, and enriched the criminal enterprises it claimed to be fighting. The evidence against it has been available, in some form, since before it began. The question of why it persists has an answer — not a comfortable one, but a clear one. It persists because the interests that benefit from its continuation are among the most powerful in the system, and because those interests have been sufficiently effective at shaping the political environment that the institutions designed to evaluate and reform policy have consistently declined to do so.

The structural conclusion

What the Full Cycle Shows

The war on drugs is valuable to this series not primarily because it is dramatic, though it is, but because it is old enough and well-documented enough to show the complete cycle that the methodology piece described: a policy enacted against the available evidence, maintained against overwhelming contrary evidence, subjected to controlled experiments whose results were clear, and defended — against all of this — by a coalition whose composition answers the question its proponents refuse to ask.

That coalition has never been monolithic and has never required coordination. It has always included the institutional self-interest of enforcement bureaucracies whose existence the policy justified. It has always included the political utility of selective criminalisation as a tool for managing communities that posed political threats. It has always included the financial interests of criminal enterprises whose profitability the policy created and sustains. And it has, for at least as long as the intelligence dimensions described above have been operative, included the covert operational interests of agencies that found the criminal infrastructure useful and the drug war a convenient screen.

Each of these interests is independently sufficient to generate political resistance to reform. Together, they constitute a coalition that has proven capable of absorbing the evidence of policy failure, domesticating the results of successful experiments, and maintaining a position that no honest reading of the data supports. The convergent evolution analogy is exact: these are not the same actors, they do not share a plan, and they do not need to. They share a niche — the political and economic space created by criminalisation — and they fill it, each in their own way, with the organisational forms the niche selects for.

Understanding this does not make reform simple. The interests arrayed against it are real, powerful, and deeply embedded in the political and financial systems this series examines. But it does make reform legible — which is the precondition for making it possible. The War on Drugs is not a policy mistake that has somehow survived. It is a policy that is working, for some of the people it was designed to work for. Seeing that clearly is the beginning of being able to change it.
