History does not have a destination. This is not a counsel of despair — it is the most important truth we can arm ourselves with as we face the particular crisis of our own time. The belief that history moves by necessity toward better arrangements, that the contradictions of each age contain within them the guaranteed seeds of a higher order, is not merely philosophically mistaken. In a period of genuine civilisational transition, it is dangerous. It is the belief that permits passivity at precisely the moment when active, intelligent choice is most urgently required.

To make this argument properly, we need to start further back than most political analysis is willing to go. Not with the twentieth century, not with the industrial revolution, but with the deep structure of how human societies develop — and how they break.

The Mode of Production as Constraint, Not Destiny

Marx and Engels proposed that the material base of society — its mode of production — determines the social, legal, and political superstructure built upon it. Read charitably, and with the refinements that Engels himself introduced in his later correspondence, this is not a claim about brute physical reality but about our technological and organisational relationship with the material world: the tools we use, the ways we cooperate to use them, the social forms that emerge from that cooperation. On this reading, historical materialism is a serious and important analytical framework. A hunter-gatherer mode of production genuinely cannot sustain a liberal democratic superstructure. The constraints are real.

But the strong version of the thesis — that the mode of production determines the superstructure, that history moves through necessary stages by an internal logic that operates independently of human choice — overstates what the evidence supports, and does so in ways that matter enormously for politics. The archaeological record of the Neolithic transition offers one of the clearest demonstrations of why.

Populations in the Fertile Crescent possessed knowledge of cultivation for millennia before adopting it as a primary subsistence strategy. As James C. Scott documents in Against the Grain (2017), drawing on the archaeobotanical and zooarchaeological record, the transition to agriculture was not a simple response to the discovery of a superior technology. In many environments, foraging was considerably less laborious and nutritionally more reliable than early farming. Marshall Sahlins' analysis of hunter-gatherer subsistence, first published in Stone Age Economics (1972) and subsequently refined by later ethnographic work, suggested that foragers in productive environments met their subsistence needs with substantially less labour than agriculturalists. The knowledge of cultivation was available; the choice to deploy it was conditioned on circumstances.

The mode of production constrains and drives social development without determining it. It defines the space of what is possible. Within that space, choice, culture, and contingency determine which possibility is realised.

The central methodological claim

What appears to have driven the transition in most regions was environmental stress — the end of the Younger Dryas climatic period around 11,700 years ago and the increased seasonality of the early Holocene created periods of scarcity in environments that had previously supported foraging reliably. Population pressure contributed in some cases; local ecological change in others. The proximate causes varied. But the functional logic was consistent: the cost-benefit calculation of cultivation shifts when the alternative is hunger. Latent capability, available for millennia, was activated by changed circumstances.

This gives us our first methodological principle. Structural conditions do not dictate outcomes; they reshape the landscape of costs and incentives within which agents make choices. The determinism is partial, not total. And this distinction carries consequences all the way through to the present.

Surplus, Predation, and the Origin of Politics

The transition to agriculture did not produce a more peaceful world. It produced, initially, a more violent one — and understanding why is essential to understanding what political institutions are actually for.

Hunter-gatherer inter-group conflict was real, but it was structurally limited. Dispersal was a survival requirement: bands had to remain small and spread across territory to avoid exhausting any location's carrying capacity. The anthropological literature on forager band organisation, synthesised by Robin Dunbar and others, shows remarkable consistency in group sizes across different cultures — typically twenty-five to fifty individuals in the primary foraging band, embedded in a wider network of perhaps one hundred and fifty to five hundred who shared language and territory. Violence between bands existed, but it operated within conventions that permitted the exchange of mates and information essential to genetic diversity and survival. There was little worth stealing beyond people.

Agriculture changed this at the root. The harvest represents concentrated, storable, portable wealth — and it is visible. A predator can watch a farmer work through a season and then take the result by force. For the first time in human history, banditry became a viable way of life. And the political problem this created — how does a settled, productive population defend itself against organised predation? — is not a peripheral question in the history of civilisation. It is, in very large measure, what the history of political organisation is.

Mancur Olson's analysis in his 1993 essay Dictatorship, Democracy, and Development identified a structural logic that is both illuminating and deeply uncomfortable. The roving bandit, who moves on after taking everything, has no incentive to leave the productive capacity of his victims intact. But the stationary bandit — one who controls a fixed territory and expects to extract from it repeatedly — has an incentive to moderate his extraction, to leave enough for next year's harvest, and eventually to provide the order and protection that maximises the long-run productivity of the population under his control. On this analysis, the earliest states emerge not as social contracts but as protection rackets that discover an interest in their clients' welfare.

I

Total Predation

The roving bandit takes everything and moves on. Short time horizon, maximum immediate extraction, no interest in preserving productive capacity. Leaves nothing behind.

II

Tributary Rule

The stationary bandit extracts a sustainable surplus. Longer time horizon generates an interest in the peasant's welfare — a productive population yields more than a devastated one.

III

Legitimate Government

The lord who provides genuine protection, dispute resolution, and infrastructure in exchange for tax and service. The interest in subjects' welfare becomes partially internalised rather than merely instrumental.

These are not stages through which societies necessarily progress. They are points on a spectrum that can be traversed in either direction. What looks like settled governance can revert toward predation when the time horizons of those in power shorten — when the costs of predation fall, when the capacity for resistance weakens, when institutional norms erode. The direction of travel is not guaranteed by any internal logic of historical development. It depends on choices, on power, and on the structural conditions that make certain choices more or less costly.

The Interregnum and Its Violence

The genetic and archaeological evidence from the period of early agricultural intensification — roughly five to seven thousand years ago across different regions — confronts us with something that optimistic theories of historical progress find difficult to accommodate.

A 2015 study by Karmin and colleagues, published in Genome Research, identified a dramatic bottleneck in male-line genetic diversity occurring across this period. The ratio of reproducing females to reproducing males implied by the Y-chromosome data is extraordinary — in some populations, the signal is consistent with something in the range of one reproducing man for every seventeen women across multiple generations. A comparable bottleneck does not appear in the mitochondrial record. This is not the signature of plague or famine, which would affect both sexes. It is the signature of systematic, organised inter-group violence eliminating male lineages while absorbing women — a pattern confirmed by the demographic profiles of massacre sites such as Schöneck-Kilianstädten in central Europe, where young women of reproductive age are conspicuously absent from the remains of what was evidently a systematic killing.

Subsequent work by Zeng and colleagues in Nature Communications (2018) refined the interpretation: the pattern is consistent with patrilineal clan groups competing violently over generations, with successful lineages eliminating the male lines of defeated groups entirely. This is the agricultural banditry problem at its most raw — before any stable institutional response has crystallised, before the roving bandit has settled, before the lord-peasant relationship has developed its grudging mutual interest. It is the interregnum: the period of maximum violence between the breakdown of one social order and the emergence of whatever comes next.

Transitions between modes of production do not reliably produce progress. They produce crises of indeterminate outcome. The old social order breaks down, and what emerges from that breakdown is not determined in advance. It could be greater complexity and order. It could be a catastrophe that eliminates the majority of a generation. It could be collapse to a simpler mode. The outcome depends on choices made under pressure by people operating with incomplete understanding of what is happening to them.

The Neolithic evidence

This is a direct challenge to the teleological confidence that runs through the Marxist inheritance from Hegel. Marx retained from his intellectual predecessor not merely the dialectical method but the conviction that history's contradictions are ultimately progressive — that each stage contains within it the seeds of a higher resolution. The suffering of each period is, on this account, the necessary labour of a larger movement toward human emancipation. The Neolithic evidence refuses this comfort. The violence of that period was not productive suffering on the road to something better. It was catastrophic, contingent, and shaped entirely by the choices and circumstances of the people living through it.

Against Waiting for the Collapse

The political consequences of teleological determinism are not merely theoretical. They are visible in the history of revolutionary movements, and they are dangerous.

The revolutions of the modern era — French, Russian, Chinese — share a common structure that is quite different from what Marx predicted. In each case the revolutionary vanguard was not an organised industrial proletariat exercising the structural power that their position in the mode of production gave them. It was an educated, ideologically motivated elite mobilising the grievances of a largely pre-industrial peasantry against a weakened and unusually rigid aristocracy. Lenin was explicit about the implication: the vanguard party was necessary precisely because the proletariat could not develop revolutionary consciousness spontaneously. This is an admission that the predicted revolutionary subject did not exist in the required form.

The result in each case was not the withering away of the state but its dramatic intensification — a new administrative and coercive elite exercising power in the name of a class whose interests it did not reliably serve. These were not the revolutions Marx predicted because the conditions Marx predicted them from did not exist. They were, rather, the form that bourgeois development took in societies where the aristocracy had been unusually tenacious — where the path to commercial and industrial modernity was blocked by a ruling class unwilling to adapt, and the emergent bourgeoisie had no choice but to come through the barricades rather than through the counting house.

Britain offers the instructive contrast. The relative flexibility of the British aristocracy — its willingness to intermarry with mercantile wealth, to invest in trade and manufacture, to absorb the emerging commercial class rather than confront it — meant that bourgeois development happened through existing institutions rather than requiring their violent overthrow. And the British working class, operating within this more permeable institutional landscape, pursued a strategy that most continental Marxists regarded with contempt: not the overthrow of the ruling class, but the progressive improvement of their position within the existing order, using the structural power that industrial production gave them.

Through the trade union movement, the cooperative movement, and ultimately parliamentary representation and the postwar welfare settlement, they achieved something real and substantial — the National Health Service, social housing, the extension of education, a genuine compression of inequality — with remarkably little violence by historical standards. The synthesis was available because the institutional landscape permitted it. The dialectic of resolution proved more productive than the dialectic of arms.

The Mode of Production as a Set of Rules

Here we reach the point the entire argument has been building toward. A mode of production is not merely a technology. It is a set of rules — about who owns what, who has power over whom, what can be bought and sold, what obligations attach to property and to position. Those rules are not natural facts. They are human choices, embedded in institutions, sustained by collective behaviour and political will.

The British labour movement understood this intuitively. Their struggle was not to smash the rules but to change them — to extend the franchise, to legalise collective bargaining, to embed the interests of working people in the institutional fabric of the state. They grasped that the mode of production constrained their options but did not eliminate them, that within the space of possibility it opened, the rules could be negotiated, the distribution of power could be shifted, the synthesis was reachable without the catastrophe.

This is the methodological position the present analysis defends. Neither the rigid determinism that sees the mode of production as the sovereign cause from which all else follows, nor the extreme anthropological particularism that refuses all generalisation and sees only the unique in every culture. Rather: structural forces define the possibility space, and within that space, choice matters. The choices people make, the institutions they build, the rules they negotiate and defend and allow to erode — these are genuine causal forces in the historical process, not epiphenomenal reflections of a deeper material necessity.

The Transition We Are Living Through

We are now in a transition between modes of production. The industrial-democratic mode that structured the twentieth century political settlement is being disrupted by something we do not yet have a settled name for — digital-informational capitalism, platform economics, AI-augmented production. The social and political superstructures built on the old mode are under strain in ways that are now visible to everyone. Democratic institutions, professional norms, the rule of law, the postwar international order: all are showing fractures.

The Marxist framework offers one diagnosis: the contradictions of capitalism are intensifying, and the crisis will produce its resolution. But this diagnosis fails at its most important point. The new mode of production does not require human labour at scale. It requires capital, computational infrastructure, and a relatively small number of highly skilled people to develop and maintain the systems. The structural power that industrial labour possessed — the ability to withdraw cooperation from a system that genuinely needed it — evaporates when cooperation is no longer required. The revolutionary subject that Marx predicted industrial capitalism was producing has not materialised. Instead, a large and growing population is being rendered progressively superfluous — not exploited, but simply not needed — and superfluous populations do not have structural power. They have, at most, the power of disruption, of disorder, of the inchoate political rage that we are already watching generate authoritarian movements across the developed world.

The wait-for-the-collapse strategy, always intellectually irresponsible, is now actively dangerous. If the Neolithic transition is any guide, collapse does not reliably produce progress. It produces a dangerous interregnum in which the worst outcomes are fully available and the best outcomes require active, intelligent work to achieve. The selection pressure that the Foundation has identified — the systematic elevation to positions of power of people skilled at short-term extraction rather than long-term governance — is now operating in a context where the constraints that previously moderated it are weakening. When labour had structural power, capital had to negotiate. When labour is superfluous, that negotiating pressure dissolves.

There is no teleological force that will navigate this transition on our behalf. If such a force exists at all, it works through us and our striving — it does not do the work for us. Good outcomes are available. So are catastrophic ones. Which we get depends on the choices we make now, while the institutional fabric still holds enough to be worth defending and reforming.

The central claim

Choosing Well

The argument this essay has traced — from the Neolithic transition through the logic of predation and protection, through the violence of agricultural intensification, through the successes and failures of modern labour movements, to the present crisis — leads to a single conclusion that is neither comfortable nor despairing.

The structural forces bearing on our current situation are real and powerful. The dissolution of labour's structural power, the acceleration of institutional capture, the emergence of a mode of production that concentrates wealth and influence with unprecedented speed — these are not problems that individual virtue or good intentions can simply overcome. Marx was right that the mode of production matters enormously. The constraints are genuine.

But the constraints do not determine the outcome. They never have. At every previous transition, within the space of possibility that structural conditions opened, human choices shaped which possibility was realised. The Neolithic interregnum produced catastrophic violence in some places and times and the emergence of durable protective institutions in others. The industrial transition produced revolutionary terror in some societies and a negotiated welfare settlement in others. The difference was not historical necessity. It was choices, institutions, political intelligence, and the willingness of enough people to understand what was happening and act on that understanding.

The mode of production is a set of rules, and rules can be changed. Not by waiting for their internal contradictions to produce automatic resolution. Not by violent rupture that destroys the institutional fabric along with the specific rules you want to change. But by the kind of deliberate, organised, politically intelligent action that takes structural forces seriously without surrendering agency to them — that understands the possibility space clearly enough to identify where within it the better outcomes lie, and works persistently toward them.

We are not the passive objects of historical forces. We are the historical forces — the ones through whom this transition will be navigated, for better or worse. The question is not whether we will shape the outcome. It is whether we will do so with understanding, or by default.
